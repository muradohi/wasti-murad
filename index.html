Business Thinking

Two projects where getting the question right mattered more than the model that answered it.

// case_study_01 · causal inference · marketing

The Campaign That Was "Working" — Wasn't

An observational marketing dataset, ~5,000 users, conversion rates between 8–13%. Exposure wasn't randomized, users were targeted by prior spend, visits, segment, channel, and country. A raw comparison would overestimate the effect. The question wasn't did the campaign work, it was for whom, and by how much.

The problem

Higher-spend and more active users were slightly more likely to be treated. Mild selection bias, not severe, distributions overlapped, but enough that a naive comparison would credit the campaign for purchases that were going to happen anyway. The standard predictive framing ("who buys?") would confidently target the wrong people: certain converters who needed no nudge. The right question was different: who buys because of us?

The impact

Average uplift across the audience was 5.75 percentage points. But the top 25–30% of users showed 11–14 points, two to three times the average. Concentrated in high-spend customers in Outdoor and Fashion, and in India and Social channels. Food, Health, and Email showed consistently low incremental effect.

The output wasn't a ranked list of likely buyers. It was a three-bucket allocation framework: target the persuadable top quartile, don't spend on certain converters who buy anyway, exclude segments with no treatment effect. That distinction is what converts a model output into a budget decision a marketing team can act on.

Read the full diagnosis
The diagnosis

I started with balance checks: comparing prior spend and prior visits between treated and control users to quantify how non-random the targeting actually was. Higher-spend users were slightly more likely to be treated, but the distributions overlapped strongly — bias was real but tractable. I formalised this with propensity score overlap and stratification by spend and propensity class to build intuition before modelling.

Then I moved to adjusted analysis. Stratified logistic regression controlling for spend, visits, channel, segment, and country surfaced where the campaign actually moved the needle: Outdoor and Fashion segments, India, Social and Video channels. Engagement scaled cleanly — treated users received 7–8× more impressions while CTR stayed stable, suggesting the lift wasn't a fatigue artefact.

Why a T-Learner

Average treatment effect tells you whether to run the campaign. It doesn't tell you who to spend on. For that I needed individual-level heterogeneity — uplift per user, not population mean. I used a T-Learner: separate outcome models for treated and control, with the difference as the estimated uplift. Simple, interpretable, and aligned with what the stratified results were already showing.

"The question 'who buys?' is the wrong question. 'Who buys because of us?' requires a different model entirely and asking it first is what determines whether the output is useful."
// the insight

Average effects mislead. The campaign "worked" but most of the value came from a quarter of the audience, and a well-run standard predictive model would have pointed budget at the wrong people with confidence. Heterogeneity isn't a nuance; it's where the ROI lives.

// case_study_02 · predictive maintenance · time series

The Engine That Looked Healthy — Wasn't

A fleet of turbofan engines, each producing multivariate sensor time-series across hundreds of cycles. No engine is identical, operating conditions vary, and degradation is gradual. The goal wasn't just to predict failure, but to decide when intervention is actually necessary.

The problem

Early in an engine’s life, predicting whether it has 200 or 250 cycles left doesn’t change any decision. Late in life, being wrong by even 10 cycles can mean failure. A standard regression framing treats all errors equally, but operationally they are not. The question wasn’t just how many cycles remain, it was when does uncertainty become critical.

The impact

The model prioritised accuracy in the late degradation phase, where decisions matter most. Instead of treating RUL as a uniform prediction problem, the system emphasised risk-aware estimation. The output was not just a number, but a prioritisation signal: which engines require attention now versus later.

Read the full diagnosis
The diagnosis

Raw sensor values fluctuate heavily due to operating conditions, masking the degradation signal. The first step was not modelling, but removing this noise through feature engineering: rolling statistics, trend slopes, and variance windows that expose gradual wear over time. This transformed the problem from noisy time-series into structured degradation patterns.

The modelling choice

Tree-based models like XGBoost performed strongly because they captured nonlinear interactions between sensors and degradation features without requiring sequence-level training. The improvement wasn’t from model complexity, but from aligning the representation with how degradation actually manifests.

"Predicting failure is easy. Predicting when the prediction matters is the real problem."
// the insight

Not all prediction errors are equal. In predictive maintenance, value comes from being right when it matters, not from being uniformly accurate. Framing the problem around decision impact, not just model performance, is what makes the system useful.
